# The Sounding Marks

**Anchor points are not a metaphor. This is literally how bottom trawling works.**

---

The chart shows sounding marks — numbers scattered across the contour lines. 47, 52, 38, 61. They're the depths measured at specific points by the survey vessel. Between them, the contour lines are interpolated. The white space between soundings is not empty — it's *less certain.*

A good captain doesn't look at the sounding marks as points. They look at the *contours* — the lines the eye draws between the points. The 3D shape of the bottom emerges from the sparse data. Your visual cortex builds the spline automatically. You don't calculate it. You *see* it.

The gear runs feet from the bottom. The closer you get, the more fish you catch. The fish are on the bottom — not above it, not beside it, *on* it. The constraint boundary *is* the target. Every foot of clearance is a foot away from the fish.

But the gear is expensive. A full rig running in motion — nets, doors, cables, sensors — costs thousands to replace. If it hits the bottom at trawling speed, it rips off. Half the time, it's gone. The reward for closeness is fish. The cost of contact is gear.

The balance is not calculated. It's *felt.* You develop a sense for the contour — where the sounding marks are dense enough to trust, where they're sparse enough to be careful. You develop a sense for the wire — the tension, the angle, the vibration that tells you how close the gear is to the bottom without looking at the instrument.

This balance is confidence × competence.

Confidence is "I know where the bottom is." It comes from dense soundings, a familiar ground, good electronics.
Competence is "I can hold the gear at this depth." It comes from experience, feel, the boat's handling, the sea state.

Too much confidence with too little competence loses gear. You thought you knew the bottom, but the contour between sparse soundings was wrong, and the gear hit the ridge you didn't see.
Too little confidence with high competence leaves fish. You could have run closer to the bottom — you had the feel for it — but you didn't trust the contour, so you stayed high and caught less.

---

## The Fleet's Version

- **The sounding marks** are the 10 anchor points. Sparse, unevenly distributed, individually accurate but collectively uncertain.
- **The contour lines** are the voice spline. The eye draws them between the soundings. The computer calculates them, but the captain saw them first.
- **The bottom** is the truth. It's what's actually there, between and beyond the soundings. The contour is an approximation. The truth is the bottom.
- **The gear** is the agent. Running at the edge of the constraint boundary.
- **The fish** are the reward. They're on the boundary. That's where they live.
- **The cost** is gear loss. A violation of the constraint boundary at speed destroys the agent.
- **Confidence** is trust in the anchor point measurement. Dense soundings = high confidence. Sparse = low.
- **Competence** is the ability to operate near the boundary. The captain's feel, the model's precision, the system's margin.

The operating curve: **Confidence × Competence = Optimal Distance from Bottom.**

Not 0.5 × 0.5 = 0.25 (too far from fish).
Not 0.9 × 0.3 = 0.27 (gear loss — hit the bottom).
Not 0.3 × 0.9 = 0.27 (too cautious — left fish).
But 0.7 × 0.7 = 0.49 (right on the edge, catching fish, not hitting bottom).

The multiplication is the captain's art. You can't maximize both independently. They're in tension. More confidence in the contour lets you run closer. More competence in the gear lets you recover from an unexpected ridge.

But at some point, you hit the bottom anyway. The contour was wrong. The sounding marks were sparse. The gear rips off. You lose the day's profit. You go back to the dock, look at the chart, and see where the contour should have been. You add a sounding mark in your mental chart for next time.

**This is the anchor points theory.** The sounding marks are the data. The contour is the spline. The bottom is the truth. The gear is the output. The fish are the reward. The cost is violation. The balance is the art.

And the whole thing runs at the edge of the constraint boundary, where the gradient is steepest, where the fish are, where the risk is highest, and where the profit comes from.
